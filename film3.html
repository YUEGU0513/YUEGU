Digital Witnesses:
Capturing Humanity in Documentary Photography
Through each frame, we explore the depth of human experience, from everyday joys to significant social
challenges, documenting the truth with authenticity and urgent clarity
· Project Introduction


In the midst of the global COVID-19 pandemic, the world’s eyes were often drawn to stark, dramatic images, painting a singular narrative of crisis and despair. Yet, life is complex and this narrow perspective fails to capture the facts. This documentary photography project, conducted during the peak and subsequent phases of the pandemic in China, seeks to broaden the lens, offering a more nuanced view of daily life beyond the crisis.
Through this project, I invite you to see China as it truly is—multifaceted, vibrant, and enduring. Each image tells a story of not just how people faced a global health crisis, but how life, in its most ordinary and extraordinary forms, continued amidst it.

Technical Tips for Storytelling

Identifying the right subjects is crucial in documentary photography. Look for individuals or communities whose stories resonate deeply with the theme you wish to explore. Engage with subjects that provide a window into the broader narrative you're telling. Remember, every subject has a story worth telling, whether it's a bustling street vendor or a quiet moment in a household. Start by observing your surroundings and be ready to capture moments that speak to the human experience in its varied forms.

Documentary photography carries a significant ethical responsibility. Always approach your subjects with respect and seek permission where possible, especially in sensitive situations. Be transparent about your intentions and how the photos will be used. It's essential to maintain the dignity of your subjects and portray them with integrity. Avoid exploiting vulnerable subjects for the sake of impactful imagery. Strive to ensure that your work contributes positively to the understanding of the context or situation you are documenting.

The technical aspects of photography--composition, lighting, and timing--are pivotal in storytelling. Use composition to guide the viewer’s eye and emphasize the story you want to tell. Natural light can dramatically enhance the mood and authenticity of your shots, so pay attention to lighting conditions and how they interact with your subjects. Timing is also critical; often, the most telling moments are fleeting, so always be ready. A well-timed photograph can capture an emotion or an event that speaks volumes.

Additional Tips:
  • Experiment with Exposure: With double exposure, getting the right exposure can be tricky. It’s often recommended to underexpose each layer slightly to prevent the final image from being overexposed.
  • Keep a Record: Especially when manually rewinding film or reusing a roll, keep detailed notes of your shots and their corresponding frames to aid in planning and execution.
Grab your camera, and let your creativity flow!
