Selecting the right camera and film is pivotal in film photography. Opt for a reliable, easy-to-carry camera that suits your everyday needs--whether it’s a classic SLR, a rangefinder, or a compact point-and-shoot. For film, consider both ISO and color palette; lower ISO films are ideal for bright days, while higher ISOs perform better in low light. Experiment with different brands to find the film that beautifully renders the colors and grains you love.

Keeping your eyes open to the beauty of mundane moments is key to daily film photography. Start by observing your routine through a new lens--maybe the morning light in your kitchen or the evening crowd at the bus stop. Carry your camera everywhere, and don't hesitate to capture what strikes you, no matter how ordinary it may seem. Sometimes, the most compelling photographs come from the simplest subjects. Also, regularly explore new routes, places, and activities to refresh your perspective and spark creativity.

Developing film can be both costly and time-consuming, but there are ways to manage these challenges. If you shoot regularly, consider learning to develop black and white film at home to cut costs significantly. For color film, bulk purchasing and developing can also be economical. Keep a consistent schedule for sending out or developing your rolls, perhaps once a week or biweekly, depending on your shooting frequency.

Additional Tips:
  • Experiment with Exposure: With double exposure, getting the right exposure can be tricky. It’s often recommended to underexpose each layer slightly to prevent the final image from being overexposed.
  • Keep a Record: Especially when manually rewinding film or reusing a roll, keep detailed notes of your shots and their corresponding frames to aid in planning and execution.
Grab your camera, and let your creativity flow!
